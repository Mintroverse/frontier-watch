Obsolescence check

Is the in-house work still the right bet?

Editorial assessment — judgment, not source data

Short answer: still the right bet — and this week it was the operators, not the vendors, who said so.

FedEx and Walmart committing networks to agents validates the operating model our pilots assume; the planning majors converging on agents-inside-the-model re-prices the governed semantic layer we already maintain. Nothing this edition threatens the in-house planning core; the connective tissue — vendor-built MCP connectors on standard rails — is arriving on schedule.

Validated by this edition

  • Customer-side commitment: the two largest US network operators scheduled agents into operations — the 'agents progress routine work, humans own exceptions' shape our SADA-plus-gates template already encodes.
  • In-model agents: Kinaxis, o9 and Blue Yonder all sell agents whose value is the planning model they reason in — the same bet as our governed semantic layer, now made by every planning vendor at once.
  • Standard rails: NetSuite's first-party MCP connector plus the release candidate's deprecation policy make 'build to the standard, not the vendor SDK' the safer integration posture.

Watch — could overlap or absorb

  • Acumatica's first-party MCP gap: if 2026 R2 doesn't answer NetSuite's move, our AI integrations sit on third-party connectors — workable, but a dependency to manage deliberately (M13).
  • Agent-mediated ordering expectations: key accounts exposed to text-a-reorder service could start expecting it from us — the cheap pilot (M8) remains the hedge.
  • Fabric planning GA rollout stays unresolved a third cycle — the potential overlap with in-house capacity/WIP planning tools from E01 remains open, unobserved again.
